## The right

New York's environmental right lives in its state constitution. **N.Y. Const. art. I,
§ 19** — the "Green Amendment," ratified in November 2021 and effective January 1,
2022 — provides:

> "Each person shall have a right to clean air and water, and a healthful
> environment."

This is a **new and developing** provision. Unlike older, well-litigated environmental
rights in other states, § 19 has not yet been built out by a settled body of New York
case law. Crucially, **whether and how § 19 is self-executing** — that is, whether it
can be enforced directly under the constitution without a separate enabling statute,
and on what terms — **is still being decided by the New York courts.** The
enforceability posture is **unsettled**. Treat anything stated here about how the right
operates as provisional and subject to change as the courts define it.

## Who can invoke it

§ 19 speaks of the right belonging to "each person." Read broadly, that language
suggests a wide pool of people who might seek to enforce the right. But who actually
has **standing** to bring a § 19 claim — a real, direct, and concrete enough stake in
a specific dispute — is **itself unsettled in New York** and is being worked out as
the courts apply the new amendment. This packet cannot tell you who qualifies; that is
a developing question of New York law that a court ultimately decides.

## What a claim must show

The honest version of the threshold: **there is not yet a settled test.** § 19 is new,
and the enforceable standard under it — what a claimant must allege and prove to make
out a violation of the right to clean air and water and a healthful environment — **is
still being defined by the New York courts.** This is **developing** law in the most
literal sense: the contours of the claim are being shaped case by case, and there is
no court-confirmed standard a reader can rely on.

What that means in practice:

- A reader **cannot rely on a fixed standard**, because one has not yet settled. Any
  description of "what a § 19 claim requires" today is provisional and may be revised
  or rejected by the courts.
- Because the standard is unsettled, even basic questions — what kind of government
  conduct § 19 reaches, what injury must be shown, how it interacts with existing
  environmental statutes and review processes — do not have confirmed answers yet.
- The right move is to find current New York authority and a New York lawyer who is
  tracking how the courts are developing this amendment, rather than to assume any
  particular test applies.

This packet deliberately does **not** state a test, because stating one would imply a

## Forum and posture

A § 19 claim would generally be a **New York state-court** matter. In broad shape, the
relief a claimant might seek is **declaratory** (a court ruling on what the
constitution requires) and/or **injunctive** (a court order directing a government
actor to do or stop doing something), brought against a **state or local government
actor** in its role touching the environment. But because the amendment is new, even
the proper **forum, defendant, and procedure** are not yet settled and are part of
what the courts are still developing. Do not treat the shape described here as fixed.

# The plain-language explainer

## What this is

There is a line in New York's constitution that gives each person a right to clean air
and water and a healthful environment. It is called the "Green Amendment" (N.Y. Const.
art. I, § 19), and it is **new** — New Yorkers voted it in at the end of 2021, and it
took effect at the start of 2022. Because it is so new, the courts are **still working
out** what it means and how it can be used. This packet explains what the right says
and, just as importantly, how much about it is **not yet settled**. It does not look at
your situation.

## What it asks of you

Here is the honest part: because § 19 is so new, there is **no settled checklist** of
what a claim has to show. What a claim under this amendment would generally need to do
is still being defined by the courts. In broad terms, a claim would likely have to:

- Point to some **government action or decision** affecting clean air, clean water, or
  a healthful environment.
- Connect that action to the **right § 19 protects** — though exactly how that
  connection must be shown is one of the things courts are still deciding.
- Be brought by someone with a real stake (**standing**) — and even who counts is
  unsettled.
- Be brought in the **right New York court**, in the **right way** — also not yet
  settled.

Read that list as a sketch of open questions, not a fixed set of requirements.

## How high the bar is

Honestly: **the bar is not yet settled.** Because § 19 is so new, the courts have not
finished deciding what a person must show to win under it. That is different from a
right where the rules are well known — here, the rules are still being written.

What that means for you: nobody can promise you that a § 19 claim will or will not
work, because the standard itself is **still developing**. This is exactly the kind of
situation where you need a lawyer who is **tracking the latest New York decisions** on
this amendment, because the answer can change as new cases come down.

## A plain example

Imagine a neighborhood concerned that a government decision will affect its air or
water, and residents wonder whether the new Green Amendment gives them a way to raise
that concern in court. In general terms, they might ask a New York lawyer whether a
§ 19 claim — asking a court to say what the constitution requires and to direct the
government accordingly — is something the courts would currently recognize, and on what
terms. This is a made-up illustration of the **kind of question** people are asking
about a brand-new right. It is not a statement that any real government did anything
wrong, it is not your facts, and it is not a prediction about how a court would rule —
because that law is still being developed.

## The clock

Claims that challenge government decisions can carry **deadlines** — there may be a
limited window to act, and that window can close for good. New York law sets those
limits, and because § 19 is new, how those limits apply to a § 19 claim may itself be
unsettled. This packet does not track any deadline and cannot tell you yours.
**Confirm the exact deadline with a New York lawyer right away**, before any window can
run.

The next step is to talk to a lawyer who does this work in New York. Good places to
