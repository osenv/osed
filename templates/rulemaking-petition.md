# Template — Administrative Rulemaking Petition

> **DRAFT SCAFFOLD — NOT A FILING.** A petition asking an agency to start, change, or repeal a rule is the lowest-barrier instrument in the OSED library: any person may file one, no court is required, and no lawyer is strictly required to submit it. That low barrier is the point — it is the natural first deployment for community use. But "able to file" is not "well-formed," and a petition that rests on a vacated rule, a dead doctrine, or an unsupported factual claim wastes the one bite at the apple. Review by counsel is strongly recommended before filing. See `../DISCLAIMER.md`.

## What this instrument does

Under the Administrative Procedure Act (5 U.S.C. § 553(e)), an interested person has the right to petition an agency to issue, amend, or repeal a rule. The agency must respond, though it has wide latitude on *timing*. If the agency sits on a petition unreasonably long, that delay can later support an "unreasonable delay" suit — which is why the petition and a later delay challenge form a two-step kit. Many states have parallel petition rights under their own administrative procedure acts.

## Required elements checklist

- [ ] Identity and interest of the petitioner
- [ ] The specific agency and official to whom the petition is directed
- [ ] The precise action requested (issue / amend / repeal a specifically identified rule)
- [ ] The legal authority that empowers or requires the agency to act
- [ ] The factual and scientific basis supporting the request
- [ ] The specific relief — ideally proposed regulatory text or a clear description of it
- [ ] A statement of why the action is needed now
- [ ] Date and petitioner signature

```
[Date]

[Agency name]
[Office / official with authority over the relevant program]
[Address]

PETITION FOR RULEMAKING UNDER [5 U.S.C. § 553(e) / agency rule / state APA cite]

Re:  Petition to [issue / amend / repeal] [identify the rule or program,
     with citation]

I.   PETITIONER AND INTEREST
     [Who the petitioner is and how they are affected by the matter —
     the interest that makes them an "interested person."]

II.  ACTION REQUESTED
     Petitioner requests that [agency] [issue a new rule / amend
     [cite] / repeal [cite]] to [state the change in one clear sentence].

III. LEGAL AUTHORITY
     [The statute that authorizes or requires the agency to act, with
     citation.] [⚠ REVIEWER: confirm the authority is current and has not
     been narrowed or superseded — run the doctrinal-currency check.]

IV.  FACTUAL AND SCIENTIFIC BASIS
     [The evidence supporting the request — data, studies, harms,
     conditions on the ground. Cite sources. ⚠ Do not assert facts or
     studies that cannot be supported; an unsupported petition is easy
     to deny.]

V.   PROPOSED RELIEF
     [Specific proposed regulatory text, or a clear description of the
     standard requested. The more specific, the harder to dismiss as vague.]

VI.  WHY ACTION IS NEEDED
     [The urgency / the consequence of inaction.]

Petitioner respectfully requests that the agency grant this petition and
initiate the requested rulemaking, and that it respond within a reasonable
time as required by law.

Respectfully submitted,

[Petitioner name and signature]
[Contact information]

on legal authority and on any factual/scientific assertions.]
```

## After filing — the two-step kit

If the agency does not respond within a reasonable time, the delay itself can become the basis for an unreasonable-delay challenge. Note that "unreasonable" is a judgment a court weighs against the agency's competing priorities and resources — it is not automatic, and how long is "too long" is a legal judgment for counsel. The Gap Analysis agent can help document the timeline; the Drafting agent can scaffold the delay complaint; but the decision to bring it belongs to a licensed attorney.
