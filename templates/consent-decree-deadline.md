# Template — Deadline/Duty-Suit Consent Decree Scaffold (Negotiated Resolution of a Citizen-Suit Failure-to-Act Case)

> **DRAFT SCAFFOLD — NOT AN AGREEMENT, NOT FOR LODGING OR ENTRY.** This is a statute-agnostic *scaffold* for the negotiated resolution of a citizen-suit failure-to-act case (e.g., a Clean Water Act §505(a)(2) / 33 U.S.C. §1365(a)(2) deadline suit). A consent decree is a **negotiated agreement** between the parties **and** a court order. This scaffold **proposes no terms** — every substantive term (the compliance-schedule dates, the fees, the no-admission framing, modification/force-majeure, dispute resolution, termination) is for the parties and their counsel to negotiate; the software proposes none of them. It is **not** an agreement until the parties agree, and **not effective** until it is lodged, published for public comment, and entered by the court. This template is a starting structure, not legal advice. See `../DISCLAIMER.md`.

## Why the form matters

A consent decree is both a **contract** between the parties and a **court order**: it binds both parties and is enforceable by the court that enters it. Because it is a negotiated agreement, the agent supplies **structure only** — every negotiated term is the parties' to set, and the software proposes none of them. And because it is a court order, the court does not rubber-stamp it: it enters the decree only **after the public-comment process** and a review for **fairness, reasonableness, and consistency with the statute** the suit was brought under. Until the court enters it, the decree binds no one.

## Negotiated-terms & comment/entry note

The compliance schedule and **every other term** in this scaffold are negotiated terms: the parties and their counsel set them, and the software proposes none of them — no date, no fee, no admission, no modification or termination term. A consent decree is also a court order, so it is **lodged, published for public comment, and entered by the court** — after a fairness, reasonableness, and statutory-consistency review — before it is effective. The software proposes no term and tracks no clock; the lodging, comment, and entry deadlines are for counsel to confirm and the court to apply.
