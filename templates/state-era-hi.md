## The right

Hawaiʻi's environmental right lives in its state constitution. **Haw. Const. art. XI,
§ 9** provides:

> "Each person has the right to a clean and healthful environment, as defined by laws
> relating to environmental quality, including control of pollution and conservation,
> protection and enhancement of natural resources."

Hawaiʻi's environmental protections under article XI run in **two distinct threads**,
and it is important not to confuse them.

**(a) The § 9 individual right is "as provided by law."** Read the words: the right is
"as defined by laws relating to environmental quality." That phrase matters. Unlike
state constitutional environmental rights that courts treat as freestanding and
self-executing (Pennsylvania's and Montana's, for example), the § 9 right is **defined
by reference to the underlying environmental-quality statutes** — its force as a
standalone claim depends on those statutes rather than on the constitutional text
operating by itself. As a freestanding individual claim, this is **developing** law:
its enforceability turns on the statutes the provision points to, and the contours of a
standalone § 9 claim are still being defined.

**(b) The article XI public-trust doctrine is established.** Separately from the § 9
individual right, article XI commits Hawaiʻi's natural resources — water above all — to
a **public trust**, with the State holding those resources in trust for the people.
This public-trust line is more developed. The leading decision is *In re Water Use
Permit Applications* (**Waiāhole**), **9 P.3d 409 (Haw. 2000)** — CURRENT (verified via
verify_citation) — which sets out Hawaiʻi's public-trust framework for water resources.
Cite *Waiāhole* only for the **public-trust** point — it does **not** establish a
standalone § 9 individual-right standard.

## Who can invoke it

§ 9 speaks of the right belonging to "each person." Read broadly, that language
suggests a wide pool of people who might seek to enforce the right. On the public-trust
side, the beneficiaries are the people of Hawaiʻi in whose interest the State holds the
resources. But who actually has **standing** to bring a particular claim — a real,
direct, and concrete enough stake in a specific dispute — is a separate question that
Hawaiʻi law governs and that a court ultimately decides. This packet cannot tell you
who qualifies.

## What a claim must show

The honest version of the threshold depends on which thread a claim travels.

For the **§ 9 individual right**, the right runs **through the environmental-quality
statutes** — it is "as provided by law." That means a claim under § 9 often depends on
a **specific underlying statute**: which environmental-quality law applies, what that
law requires, and how § 9 connects to it. The **standalone § 9 individual-right
standard is still developing**, and a reader cannot rely on a fixed, court-confirmed
test for a freestanding § 9 claim divorced from a statute.

For the **public-trust** claim, the contours are more developed — *Waiāhole* supplies a
framework treating the State as trustee of public natural resources (water most
clearly) and describing the trustee's duties. A public-trust claim generally argues
that a government actor, in a specific decision, fell short of those trustee duties as
to a public natural resource. Even so, apply that framework **carefully**: how it
extends across resources and decisions is fact-specific, and the public-trust line is
distinct from the § 9 individual right.

## Forum and posture

A claim under article XI would generally be a **Hawaiʻi state-court** matter. In broad
shape, the relief a claimant might seek is **declaratory** (a court ruling on what the
constitution or the underlying statutes require) and/or **injunctive** (a court order
directing a government actor to do or stop doing something), brought against a **state
or local government actor** in its role touching the environment or public natural
resources. Because the § 9 right runs through the environmental-quality statutes, much
of the action may occur in **agency proceedings under the relevant statutes** (for
example, water-use or land-use processes) rather than as a freestanding constitutional
suit. The right forum, the proper defendant, and the procedural route all depend on the
specific facts.

# The plain-language explainer

## What this is

There is a line in Hawaiʻi's constitution that gives each person a right to "a clean and
healthful environment" (Haw. Const. art. XI, § 9). But that line adds three important
words: the right is **"as defined by laws"** about environmental quality — meaning it
leans on Hawaiʻi's environmental statutes rather than standing entirely on its own.
Separately, article XI also says Hawaiʻi's natural resources — especially water — are
held by the State in a **public trust** for the people, and that part of the law is more
settled. This packet explains both threads and how much about the individual right is
**still being worked out**. It does not look at your situation.

## What it asks of you

In broad terms, a claim under this part of the constitution would generally have to:

- Point to a **specific government action or decision** affecting the environment or a
  public natural resource.
- For an individual § 9 concern, connect that action to the **environmental-quality
  statute** the right depends on — because § 9 is "as provided by law," there is often a
  specific statute doing the real work.
- For a public-trust concern, explain how a government actor fell short of its duties as
  **trustee** of a public natural resource (water being the clearest example).
- Be brought by someone with a real stake (**standing**), in the **right Hawaiʻi forum**
  — which may be a court or an **agency proceeding** under the relevant statute — in the
  **right way**.

Read that list as a sketch, not a fixed checklist — especially for the individual right,
where the standard is still being defined.

## How high the bar is

Honestly: it depends on which thread you are on, and one of them is still developing.

The **individual § 9 right** comes with those three words — **"as provided by law."**
That means it is **not** a freestanding right you can stand on by itself; it leans on
Hawaiʻi's environmental-quality statutes, and the standalone individual-right standard
is **still being defined**. You cannot rely on a fixed test here, because the law is
still developing and often depends on a specific statute.

The **public-trust** thread is more settled — Hawaiʻi courts have built out a framework
(especially for water) describing the State's duties as trustee. But it still has to be
applied carefully to the specific resource and decision, and it is a different thing
from the individual § 9 right.

Either way, this is the kind of matter that needs a lawyer who does Hawaiʻi
environmental law and is tracking how this developing area stands today.

## A plain example

Imagine a community concerned that a government decision will affect a local water
source, and residents wonder whether Hawaiʻi's constitution gives them a way to raise
that concern. In general terms, they might ask a Hawaiʻi lawyer whether the **public
trust** over water supports a claim that the State, as trustee, did not weigh its duties
before acting — and, separately, whether any **environmental-quality statute** the § 9
right depends on is in play. This is a made-up illustration of the **shape** of such a
question. It is not a statement that any real government did anything wrong, it is not
your facts, and it is not a prediction about how a court would rule — especially as to
the individual right, where the law is still developing.

## The clock

Claims that challenge government decisions can carry **deadlines** — there may be a
limited window to act, and that window can close for good. This is especially true of
**agency proceedings**, which often have short, strict windows to participate or to
appeal. Hawaiʻi law sets those limits, and they depend on what is being challenged and
how. This packet does not track any deadline and cannot tell you yours. **Confirm the
exact deadline with a Hawaiʻi lawyer right away**, before any window can run.
