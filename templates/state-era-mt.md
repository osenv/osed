## The right

Montana's environmental right lives in its state constitution, in two provisions that
work together. The first names the right itself. **Mont. Const. art. II, § 3** (part
of Montana's Declaration of Rights) provides:

> "All persons are born free and have certain inalienable rights. They include the
> right to a clean and healthful environment and the rights of pursuing life's basic
> necessities..."

The second imposes a duty to back the right up. **Mont. Const. art. IX, § 1**
provides:

> "The state and each person shall maintain and improve a clean and healthful
> environment in Montana for present and future generations."

The posture is distinctive. Because the right to a clean and healthful environment
sits in art. II, § 3 alongside Montana's other Declaration-of-Rights guarantees, it is
treated as a **self-executing fundamental right** — enforceable directly under the
constitution, without waiting for the legislature to pass a statute to carry it out.
Because it is a *fundamental* right, **state action that infringes it is reviewed under
strict scrutiny** — the most demanding standard courts use, under which the state must
show a compelling interest and a closely tailored means. Art. IX, § 1 adds an
**affirmative duty**: the state, and each person, must maintain and improve the
environment for present and future generations.

This is **developed** doctrine in Montana. Two decisions anchor its modern shape:
*Montana Environmental Information Center v. Department of Environmental Quality*
(MEIC), 988 P.2d 1236 (Mont. 1999) — CURRENT (verified via verify_citation) —
established the fundamental-right / strict-scrutiny framework; and *Held v. State*, 560
P.3d 1235 (Mont. 2024) — CURRENT (verified via verify_citation) — the affirmed
youth-climate decision applying the right.

## Who can invoke it

Art. II, § 3 frames the right as belonging to "all persons," and art. IX, § 1 phrases
the duty as running to "present and future generations." As a general matter, that is
the pool of people the right is meant to protect. Whether a particular person or group
has **standing** to bring a particular claim — a real, direct, and concrete enough
stake in the specific dispute — is a separate, fact-specific question that Montana law
governs and that a court ultimately decides.

## What a claim must show

The honest version of the threshold: the right is real, enforceable, and backed by a
demanding standard of review — but a claim is not automatic, and meeting the standard
takes work.

Because the right to a clean and healthful environment is a **fundamental** right,
*MEIC* holds that **state action affecting the environment is reviewed under strict
scrutiny**. In practice that means a claim generally argues that some specific state
action (or a statute that constrains the state's environmental review) implicates the
right, which shifts the burden to the state to justify the action under that demanding
standard — to show a compelling state interest pursued by narrowly tailored means.

The **access point** is that the right is self-executing and reaches state conduct
directly — a claimant does not have to find a separate statute that was broken — and
the strict-scrutiny standard is genuinely state-favoring to overcome. The **hard
parts** are real: a claimant must identify the **specific state action** at issue,
show that it implicates the environmental right rather than merely touches the
environment, show a concrete enough injury and stake to have **standing**, fit the
claim to the **correct forum and procedure**, and reckon with how Montana courts have
applied and continue to refine the framework (including how *Held* applies it) to the
kind of action involved. None of that is a formality.

## Forum and posture

A claim under these provisions is a **Montana state-court** matter, typically brought
in **District Court**. The relief usually sought is **declaratory** (a court ruling on
what the constitution requires) and/or **injunctive** (a court order directing a state
actor to do or stop doing something), brought against a **state actor or agency** in
its role touching the environment. The proper court, the proper defendant, and the
procedural route all depend on the specific facts.

# The plain-language explainer

## What this is

There are two lines in Montana's constitution that, together, give the people a right
to a clean and healthful environment and put a duty on the state — and on each person —
to maintain and improve it for everyone, including people not yet born. The right is in
Montana's Declaration of Rights (art. II, § 3); the duty is in art. IX, § 1. Montana
courts treat the right as a real, enforceable, *fundamental* right — which means state
action that infringes it gets the most demanding kind of court review. This packet
explains what that right is and what a claim under it generally has to show. It does
not look at your situation.

## What it asks of you

To stand on this right, a claim generally has to:

- Point to a **specific state action or decision** — by a state actor or agency — that
  affects the environment in a way that implicates the right.
- Explain how that action infringes the **right to a clean and healthful environment**,
  or falls short of the state's duty to maintain and improve it.
- Show that the person or group bringing it has a real, direct stake in the dispute
  (**standing**).
- Be brought in the **right Montana court**, against the **right state defendant**, in
  the **right way**.

## How high the bar is

Honestly: the standard of review is strong, the access point is good, and the hard
parts are still real.

The strong part — Montana's right to a clean and healthful environment is a
**fundamental right**, so when state action infringes it, courts apply **strict
scrutiny**: the state has to show a compelling reason and a closely tailored means.
That is a demanding standard for the state to meet, and it is a real feature of Montana
law — but it is the legal *framework* a claim runs through, not a promise about how any
particular case comes out.

The good part — the right is **self-executing**, so you do not have to first find some
separate statute that was broken; the constitution itself is the source of the right.

The hard parts — you still have to tie the claim to a **specific state action**, show
it actually **implicates the right**, show a **concrete enough stake** to have
standing, and fit it to the **correct court and procedure**. None of that is a
formality. This is the kind of claim that needs a lawyer who does Montana environmental
law.

## A plain example

Imagine a state agency issues a permit for an activity that a nearby community believes
will degrade local water or air, and the community believes the agency's review skipped
the environmental harm the constitution protects against. In general terms, residents
might explore a claim arguing that the state action implicates their right to a clean
and healthful environment — which, if the right is implicated, would require the state
to justify the action under a demanding standard — asking a Montana court to declare
what the constitution requires and to direct the agency accordingly. This is a made-up
illustration of the *shape* of such a claim. It is not a statement that any real agency
did anything wrong, and it is not about your facts.

## The clock

Claims like this can carry **deadlines** — there may be a limited window to challenge a
particular state decision, and that window can close for good. Montana law sets those
limits, and they depend on what is being challenged and how. This packet does not track
any deadline and cannot tell you yours. **Confirm the exact deadline with a Montana
lawyer right away**, before any window can run.
