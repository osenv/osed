## The right

Pennsylvania's environmental right lives in its state constitution. **Pa. Const.
art. I, § 27** (the Environmental Rights Amendment) provides:

> "The people have a right to clean air, pure water, and to the preservation of the
> natural, scenic, historic and esthetic values of the environment."

The provision is understood to be **self-executing** — meaning it can be enforced
directly under the constitution without waiting for the legislature to pass a statute
to carry it out. It also carries a **public-trust** dimension: the same section names
Pennsylvania's public natural resources as the common property of the people,
including future generations, and makes the **Commonwealth the trustee** of those
resources, with a trustee's duties to conserve and maintain them.

This is **developed** doctrine in Pennsylvania. Two decisions anchor its modern shape:
*Pennsylvania Environmental Defense Foundation v. Commonwealth*, 161 A.3d 911 (Pa.
2017) — CURRENT (verified via verify_citation) — sets out the modern public-trust
standard the courts apply; and *Robinson Township v. Commonwealth*, 83 A.3d 901 (Pa.
2013) — CURRENT (verified via verify_citation) — revitalized § 27 and reframed it as
a substantive, enforceable right rather than a mere statement of policy.

## Who can invoke it

§ 27 speaks of the right belonging to "the people," and the public-trust half of the
section names the people — including future generations — as the beneficiaries of the
trust the Commonwealth holds. As a general matter, that is the pool of people who may
seek to enforce the right and the trust. Whether a particular person or group has
**standing** to bring a particular claim — a real, direct, and concrete enough stake
in the specific dispute — is a separate, fact-specific question that Pennsylvania law
governs and that a court ultimately decides.

## What a claim must show

The honest version of the threshold: § 27 is real and enforceable, but a claim is not
automatic, and meeting the standard takes work.

For the **public-trust** side, *PEDF* frames the government as a **trustee** of public
natural resources. A trustee owes duties — broadly, to conserve and maintain the trust
resources and to act with prudence, loyalty, and impartiality toward the
beneficiaries (the people, present and future). A claim on this side generally argues
that a government actor, in some specific decision or action, failed one of those
trustee duties as to a public natural resource.

The **access point** is that § 27 is self-executing and reaches government conduct
directly — a claimant does not have to find a separate statute that was broken. The
**hard parts** are real: a claimant must identify the specific government action and
the specific trustee duty or environmental value at stake, must show a concrete enough
injury and stake to have standing, must fit the claim to the correct forum and
procedure, and must reckon with how courts have applied (and continue to refine) the
*PEDF* standard to the kind of resource and decision involved. The standard is
doctrine that is still being worked out case by case.

## Forum and posture

A § 27 claim is a **Pennsylvania state-court** matter. Depending on who the defendant
is and what is being challenged, it may belong in the **Commonwealth Court** (which
hears many cases against state government) or in a county **court of common pleas**.
The relief typically sought is **declaratory** (a court ruling on what the
constitution requires) and/or **injunctive** (a court order directing a government
actor to do or stop doing something), brought against a **state or local government
actor** in its role touching public natural resources. The right forum, the proper
defendant, and the procedural route all depend on the specific facts.

# The plain-language explainer

## What this is

There is a line in Pennsylvania's constitution that gives the people a right to clean
air, pure water, and a preserved environment — and it says the state government holds
Pennsylvania's public natural resources in trust for everyone, including people not
yet born. It is called the Environmental Rights Amendment (Pa. Const. art. I, § 27).
Pennsylvania courts treat it as a real, enforceable right, not just a nice statement.
This packet explains what that right is and what a claim under it generally has to
show. It does not look at your situation.

## What it asks of you

To stand on this right, a claim generally has to:

- Point to a **specific government action or decision** — by a state or local
  government actor — that touches a public natural resource or an environmental value
  the section protects.
- Explain how that action falls short of the government's duties as **trustee** of
  those resources, or of the right the section guarantees.
- Show that the person or group bringing it has a real, direct stake in the dispute
  (**standing**).
- Be brought in the **right Pennsylvania court**, against the **right government
  defendant**, in the **right way**.

## How high the bar is

Honestly: the access point is good, and the hard parts are real.

The good part — § 27 is **self-executing**, so you do not have to first find some
separate statute that was broken; the constitution itself is the source of the right.
Pennsylvania courts have spent the last decade building this doctrine out, so it is
live and usable.

The hard parts — you have to tie the claim to a **specific government action**, show a
**concrete enough stake** to have standing, fit it to the **correct court and
procedure**, and meet a **public-trust standard that courts are still refining** case
by case. None of that is a formality. This is the kind of claim that needs a lawyer
who does Pennsylvania environmental law.

## A plain example

Imagine a state agency leases a piece of public forest land for an activity, and a
nearby community believes the decision ignored the state's duty to conserve that
public resource for everyone. In general terms, residents might explore a § 27
public-trust claim arguing the agency, as trustee, did not weigh its conservation
duties before acting — asking a Pennsylvania court to declare what the constitution
requires and to direct the agency accordingly. This is a made-up illustration of the
*shape* of such a claim. It is not a statement that any real agency did anything
wrong, and it is not about your facts.

## The clock

Claims like this can carry **deadlines** — there may be a limited window to challenge
a particular government decision, and that window can close for good. Pennsylvania law
sets those limits, and they depend on what is being challenged and how. This packet
does not track any deadline and cannot tell you yours. **Confirm the exact deadline
with a Pennsylvania lawyer right away**, before any window can run.
