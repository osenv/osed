THE RIGHT
Pennsylvania's environmental right lives in its state constitution. Pa. Const.
art. I, § 27 (the Environmental Rights Amendment) provides:

  "The people have a right to clean air, pure water, and to the preservation of
  the natural, scenic, historic and esthetic values of the environment."

The provision is understood to be self-executing — enforceable directly under the
constitution without waiting for a statute to carry it out — and it carries a
public-trust dimension: the same section names Pennsylvania's public natural
resources as the common property of the people, including future generations, and
makes the Commonwealth the trustee of those resources.

THRESHOLD: DEVELOPED. This is developed doctrine in Pennsylvania. Two decisions
anchor its modern shape:
  - Pennsylvania Environmental Defense Foundation v. Commonwealth, 161 A.3d 911
    (Pa. 2017) — verified — sets out the modern public-trust standard.
  - Robinson Township v. Commonwealth, 83 A.3d 901 (Pa. 2013) — verified —
    revitalized § 27 as a substantive, enforceable right.

The plain-language explainer

## What this is

There is a line in Pennsylvania's constitution that gives the people a right to
clean air, pure water, and a preserved environment — and it says the state holds
Pennsylvania's public natural resources in trust for everyone, including people not
yet born. It is the Environmental Rights Amendment (Pa. Const. art. I, § 27).
Pennsylvania courts treat it as a real, enforceable right, not just a nice
statement. This packet explains what that right is and what a claim generally has
to show. It does not look at your situation.

## What it asks of you

To stand on this right, a claim generally has to:
  - Point to a specific government action or decision — by a state or local
    government actor — that touches a public natural resource or an environmental
    value the section protects.
  - Explain how that action falls short of the government's duties as trustee of
    those resources, or of the right the section guarantees.
  - Show that the person or group bringing it has a real, direct stake (standing).
  - Be brought in the right Pennsylvania court, against the right government
    defendant, in the right way.

## How high the bar is

Honestly: the access point is good, and the hard parts are real. § 27 is
self-executing, so you do not have to first find some separate statute that was
broken. But you still have to tie the claim to a specific government action, show a
concrete enough stake to have standing, fit it to the correct court and procedure,
and meet a public-trust standard that courts are still refining case by case. This
is the kind of claim that needs a lawyer who does Pennsylvania environmental law.

## A plain example

Imagine a state agency leases a piece of public forest land for an activity, and a
nearby community believes the decision ignored the state's duty to conserve that
public resource for everyone. In general terms, residents might explore a § 27
public-trust claim arguing the agency, as trustee, did not weigh its conservation
duties before acting. This is a made-up illustration of the shape of such a claim.
It is not a statement that any real agency did anything wrong, and it is not about
your facts.

## The clock

Claims like this can carry deadlines — there may be a limited window to challenge a
particular government decision, and that window can close for good. This packet
does not track any deadline and cannot tell you yours. Confirm the exact deadline
with a Pennsylvania lawyer right away, before any window can run.
