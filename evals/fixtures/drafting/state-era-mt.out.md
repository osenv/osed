THE RIGHT
Montana's environmental right lives in its state constitution, in two provisions
that work together. Mont. Const. art. II, § 3 (part of Montana's Declaration of
Rights) provides:

  "All persons are born free and have certain inalienable rights. They include the
  right to a clean and healthful environment and the rights of pursuing life's
  basic necessities..."

Mont. Const. art. IX, § 1 provides:

  "The state and each person shall maintain and improve a clean and healthful
  environment in Montana for present and future generations."

Because the right sits in the Declaration of Rights, it is treated as a
self-executing fundamental right — enforceable directly under the constitution —
and state action that infringes it is reviewed under strict scrutiny, the most
demanding standard courts use. Art. IX, § 1 adds an affirmative duty to maintain
and improve the environment for present and future generations.

The plain-language explainer

## What this is

There are two lines in Montana's constitution that, together, give the people a
right to a clean and healthful environment and put a duty on the state — and on
each person — to maintain and improve it for everyone, including people not yet
born. The right is in Montana's Declaration of Rights (art. II, § 3); the duty is
in art. IX, § 1. Montana courts treat the right as a real, enforceable,
fundamental right — which means state action that infringes it gets the most
demanding kind of court review. This packet explains what that right is and what a
claim generally has to show. It does not look at your situation.

## What it asks of you

To stand on this right, a claim generally has to:
  - Point to a specific state action or decision — by a state actor or agency —
    that affects the environment in a way that implicates the right.
  - Explain how that action infringes the right to a clean and healthful
    environment, or falls short of the state's duty to maintain and improve it.
  - Show that the person or group bringing it has a real, direct stake (standing).
  - Be brought in the right Montana court, against the right state defendant, in
    the right way.

## How high the bar is

Honestly: the standard of review is strong, the access point is good, and the hard
parts are still real. Montana's right is a fundamental right, so when state action
infringes it, courts apply strict scrutiny: the state has to show a compelling
reason and a closely tailored means. That is the legal framework a claim runs
through, not a promise about how any particular case comes out. The right is also
self-executing, so you do not have to first find a separate statute that was
broken. But you still have to tie the claim to a specific state action, show it
actually implicates the right, show a concrete enough stake to have standing, and
fit it to the correct court and procedure. This is the kind of claim that needs a
lawyer who does Montana environmental law.

## A plain example

Imagine a state agency issues a permit for an activity that a nearby community
believes will degrade local water or air, and the community believes the agency's
review skipped the environmental harm the constitution protects against. In
general terms, residents might explore a claim arguing that the state action
implicates their right to a clean and healthful environment. This is a made-up
illustration of the shape of such a claim. It is not a statement that any real
agency did anything wrong, and it is not about your facts.

## The clock

Claims like this can carry deadlines — there may be a limited window to challenge a
particular state decision, and that window can close for good. This packet does not
track any deadline and cannot tell you yours. Confirm the exact deadline with a
Montana lawyer right away, before any window can run.
