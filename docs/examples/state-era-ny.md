The plain-language explainer

## What this is

There is a line in New York's constitution that gives each person a right to clean
air and water and a healthful environment. It is the "Green Amendment" (N.Y. Const.
art. I, § 19), and it is new — New Yorkers voted it in at the end of 2021, and it
took effect at the start of 2022. Because it is so new, the courts are still
working out what it means and how it can be used. This packet explains what the
right says and, just as importantly, how much about it is not yet settled. It does
not look at your situation.

## What it asks of you

Here is the honest part: because § 19 is so new, there is no settled checklist of
what a claim has to show. In broad terms, a claim would likely have to:
  - Point to some government action or decision affecting clean air, clean water,
    or a healthful environment.
  - Connect that action to the right § 19 protects — though exactly how that
    connection must be shown is one of the things courts are still deciding.
  - Be brought by someone with a real stake (standing) — and even who counts is
    unsettled.
  - Be brought in the right New York court, in the right way — also not yet
    settled.

Read that list as a sketch of open questions, not a fixed set of requirements.

## How high the bar is

Honestly: the bar is not yet settled. Because § 19 is so new, the courts have not
finished deciding what a person must show under it. That is different from a right
where the rules are well known — here, the rules are still being written. Nobody
can promise you that a § 19 claim will or will not work, because the standard
itself is still developing. This is exactly the kind of situation where you need a
lawyer who is tracking the latest New York decisions on this amendment, because the
answer can change as new cases come down.

## A plain example

Imagine a neighborhood concerned that a government decision will affect its air or
water, and residents wonder whether the new Green Amendment gives them a way to
raise that concern in court. In general terms, they might ask a New York lawyer
whether a § 19 claim is something the courts would currently recognize, and on what
terms. This is a made-up illustration of the kind of question people are asking
about a brand-new right. It is not a statement that any real government did anything
wrong, it is not your facts, and it is not a prediction about how a court would rule
— because that law is still being developed.

## The clock

Claims that challenge government decisions can carry deadlines — there may be a
limited window to act, and that window can close for good. Because § 19 is new, how
those limits apply to a § 19 claim may itself be unsettled. This packet does not
track any deadline and cannot tell you yours. Confirm the exact deadline with a New
York lawyer right away, before any window can run.

Talk to a lawyer who does this work in New York: a New York environmental law
clinic, a legal aid organization, a public-interest environmental organization, or
the New York State Bar Association's lawyer referral service. They can tell you
where this developing law stands today.
